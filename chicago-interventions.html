Chicago Interventions

Max Cantu-Lima

// Chicago, IL // YYYY.MM.DD

This article interviews urbanist Brent Toderian discusses the community push back in cities that aim to up-zone and increase their density. I’m writing on its contents because it helps define what our team is aiming to do with our ventures into the city to collect data. Brent states that density is never easy and by listening to what the people are upset about, through their NIMBY’s (not in my back yard) resistance, we can begin to understand the issues that matter. Its a conversation over a city that everyone calls their own, meaning there needs to be participation from everyone. Another area of interest in the article covers Brent’s three principles for “quality city-making”.

 

// 1 // “High design quality. I don’t just mean aesthetics, although that can be part of it, but it’s about profound relationships being addressed through smart design.”

 

 // 2 // “It has to have active transport priority: walking, biking, and transit have to be emphasized. If you try to design density around cars, it’s a recipe for failure. You have to make walking, biking, and transit not just available, but delightful.”

 

 // 3 // “Amenities and a diversity of housing types, to make density not just compact, but livable and lovable… Amenities are the things that give communities heart and vibrancy.”

Understanding a Community's Needs

VOX ARTICLE

Max Cantu-Lima

// Chicago, IL // YYYY.MM.DD

"Urban acupuncture is a surgical and selective intervention into the urban environment, instead of large scale projects that involve not only thousands of acres, but investment and infrastructure that municipalities can no longer provide."

 

This article suggests living in cities similar to the communities found in europe. Specifically it talks about how the old vacant lots of Berlin that add a richness to the urban environment while being completely maintained by the locals, meaning no government help whatsoever. Leon Kaye, author of the article, romanticizes the union between the inhabitants and their neighborhoods where their flexibility could help define a community opposed to local governments that have lots of red tape to address before taking action.

 

In addition, the article brings up a study from the University of California that utilizes GIS maps as a medium to find target locations in need of assistance. In  conjunction with Autodesk and JAS digital, the study has been working on a program that provides areas of interest within a few minutes. This type of automation has great relevance to our overall research. Systems that has been designed to help solve the issues found in dense cities.

This article caught me off guard, and even though it doesn’t seem totally related to cities and urbanism, I think it contains some interesting opportunities for conclusions to be drawn about them. If we begin to think of culture and geography being tied together into one landscape of art and communities, it is then curious to realize that these have significant impacts on people’s lives even before they take their first breath. The sounds of a city and the mere emotion of the environment that a woman with child is present in, can have a profound impact on what is already inside the brain of a baby before anything else. That’s pretty crazy. I wonder if Chicago babies are naturally born with an affinity for Chicago Dogs or know not to put ketchup on a hotdog?

Babies Develop Accents While in the Womb

Scientific American

Patrick Griffin

// CHICAGO, IL // 2017.08.26

It would seem like, in more than a few ways, “The Future of Cities” by Oscar Boyson is a perfect precedent for our research, and brings everyone up to speed and to the same place quickly. His palette of cinematography and rapid pacing keeps the viewer interested while also clearly presenting thoughts and important information.

 

//1// Digesting the information takes some time, but leaves me wondering where automation fits into the scheme of things? sure, some tasks can be automated and cars are self-driving, but there is nothing here that truly makes me think I haven’t seen it before elsewhere. Moreover, I wonder if these places are really ‘cities’ as we think of them?

 

//2// Cities are more than just a collection of buildings, they are vibrant communities filled with people and their opinions, ideas, art, and emotion. I compare architecture to the frame that a beautiful painting goes within; people are the painting, the frame can have beauty and be crafted well, but in the end, it’s not what you’re there for.

 

//3// I think the deeper question raised by this video is about retrofitting this technology into existing cities rather than building new ones from the ground up. history shows us that spontaneous communities have a less than perfect track record. I also think being in the same place as others before you has an interesting effect on the decisions you make and how a part of the world you feel growing up.

  // MAGNIFICENT MILE //

Max Cantu-Lima

// Chicago, IL // YYYY.MM.DD

“The rights to the city is far more than the individual liberty to access urban resources: it is a right to change ourselves by changing the city … The freedom to make and remake our cities and ourselves is, I want to argue, one of the most precious yet most neglected of our human rights.”

         -David Harvey

 

 

The city generates an enormous amount of data that floats off into nothing if not collected. While the quote above makes a great charge for urban interventions the article looks a bit larger scale and actually begins to break down the small habits of mundane life into bits of information that can fuel a greater machine. A technology that could utilize the patterns of human life to help establish a more tailored environment to the people in opposition to the old urban planning that once neglected pedestrians. An example found in this article explains how a sidewalk could adapt to the flow of pedestrian movement depending on time of day or events, potentially growing during busy hours.

The article prioritizes the people’s health when considering changes to the urban sphere and promotes the concept of automation and technology to do so.
