Denver Interventions

Tanner Hyland

// DENVER, CO // 2017.09.05

As architects attempt to guide our cities into the age of technology while simultaneously juggling the strains of an ever-increasing need for population density, the discussion of automation in architecture and whether or not we should embrace it has become quite relevant.  While the potential for the integration of digital systems to create “smart” cities and buildings is seemingly infinite, some fear that such innovations could eventually phase out the need for architects altogether.

 

“Algorithm vs. Architect: Will Machines Design the Cities of the Future?” addresses this fear, ultimately alleging that while computerized systems have evolved beyond their original scope of mechanical, repetitive tasks to operate at a higher cognitive level, the architectural profession as a whole stands little chance of becoming obsolete.  With this in mind, it is critical to consider the ways in which our profession could be transformed in the near future, potentially providing architects with a bevy of tools to both design and implement truly sustainable futures for urban environments.

ARCHIPRENEUR ARTICLE

// Denver, CO // 2017.09.12

Food Trucks in the Park

Denver’s Civic Center Park exists in the minds of most as a missed opportunity for a shared communal space at the heart of downtown, instead offering views of the seemingly endless renovation to the State Capitol building and serving as a point of refuge for the city’s less fortunate.  However, on a weekly basis throughout the summer and fall, the park’s rightful status as a vibrant public space is restored via the presence of great food and live music; offering Denver citizens from all walks of life the chance to enjoy the park once again.  As designers, we can’t underestimate the role of informal, pop-up events such as this in activating public spaces and cultivating camaraderie within a city.

Tanner Hyland

// DENVER, CO // 2017.09.05

How can we continue to provide necessary moments of relief within urban landscapes while striving to accommodate inevitable increases in population density in nearly every major city?  Cooke Fawcett Architects have provided their answer to this question through their design of the Peckham Observatory in South London.

 

Located on the roof of what used to be a parking garage in the city’s center, the observatory is the latest in a series of interventions seeking to transform the garage into a vibrant public space within a dense urban environment.  Capitalizing on premium views of the London skyline, the rooftop is populated with a number of interactive elements ranging from experiential art to a pair of large steel staircases providing access to a viewing platform.  Realized by a relatively small budget, this project serves as an excellent example of the creative thinking necessary to preserve moments of relief within urban districts by reclaiming otherwise overlooked or underutilized spaces.  It is spaces such as these that ultimately define the culture and character of a city, providing the impetus for citizens to interact with one another and foster a lasting sense of place within their communities.

POP UP CITY ARTICLE

Tanner Hyland

// LAWRENCE, KS // YYYY.MM.DD

For each of the past seven years the CRUSH Street Art Festival has served as a unifying force for the RiNo area, bringing together local and internationally known artists to take over the district’s walls and alleyways for an entire week.  The result is an exuberant display of graffitied surfaces that transforms the ways in which people interact with their surroundings, deconstructing our understanding of the built environment as a series of fixed objects by demonstrating how the mundane can be repurposed as a dynamic work of art.

Tanner Hyland

// DENVER, CO // 2017.09.06

I recently had the opportunity to tour Panasonic’s Denver headquarters, catching a glimpse of some of the autonomous technologies they hope to deploy in the near future with the goal of making our cities smarter and more responsive.  Among their current projects is a street light system that will enable cities to detect the movement patterns of vehicles and pedestrians via a network of sensors, while simultaneously performing the added function of improving response times to inclement weather by measuring street temperatures to determine the optimal time to deploy salt trucks and snow plows.

 

While Panasonic is developing a number of innovations on this scale, perhaps their most transformational project comes in the form of an autonomous bus system being curated in conjunction with Easy Mile, a French company that has developed several prototypes for driverless vehicles.  With hopes of deploying the buses as part of a master planning effort by Gensler, it will be exciting to explore the viability of driverless vehicles in our current urban fabric and their potential impact on our cities moving forward.

Reclaiming the River

Located along an otherwise underutilized stretch of the Platte River, Confluence Park has brought Class II rapids to the heart of downtown Denver, providing opportunities for residents to engage with their surroundings in a new and exciting way.  Such a project serves as an excellent example of how the creative repurposing of neglected resources can enhance urban areas.
