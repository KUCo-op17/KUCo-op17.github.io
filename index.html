Cities are more than a collection of buildings.

In order to understand what a city will look like in the future, we need to understand the present. Today is tomorrow's context, and time never stops. The first important piece of information to understand is that cities are more than just the physical parts they are composed of. When we walk out our front doors we are immediately audience to the physical manifestations of human decisions and culture. When we call an Uber, or play Pokemon Go, we are interacting with a network and platform that has spacial elements but exist on a completely different layer than our physical world.

 

 

How can we understand the elements of a city, if they exists for different lengths of time and some are completely intangible?

The Anatomy of an Urban Space

During the early phases of our research, we realized that when looking at the world, we could use about four different "lenses" and separate out the elements of the city into fairly distinct "layers" to better understand how they interact. In this way, we can then use tools that we have learned about defining physical space to better draw conclusions about the less visible or tangible elements of an urban space.

00- Physical Space

"Ground level" - this is the geography we studied in school and the things we see outside. Buildings, Mountains, Rivers, and anything that is static on the surface of the earth.

01 - Organisms/People

"Level one" - all living things are a part of this layer, we could call it the "biosphere" or something else, but anything that is living exists on this layer, it can cause serious change to the world.

02 - Influence/Policy/Culture

"Level two" - This is where our laws and policy live. They are not living things, but can affect the lives in similar and critical ways. Government, Social Constructs, and other human hierarchical intangible structures exist on this layer. We could call this layer, "human decisions" if we wanted to.

03 - Information/Digital

"Level three" - This is the least tangible layer and the farthest from the physical world. Since the dawn of language we have been able to communicate and share information about places. In this way, places existed outside of physical location. Now we have the Internet, and this layer is made visible.

What happens when an entity bridges layers?

Master plans, infrastructure, parks, bike-lanes, are all examples of planning and design decisions people before us have made to augment how we experience our environment. These are big, expensive, decisions and they’re easy to see once you know how to look for them. But we’d be foolish to think that only people with official titles can make these decisions that impact how we experience the world around us.  Quite often, people without a title or official power, do just that, by taking things into their own hands making their community the way they want it to be.

Space-Time.

A Performance - a human based act or demonstration

Example: The parade from Ferris Bueller's Day Off

(really short term)

 

An Installation - a temporary augmentation to a space

Example: A Farmer's Market or Art Walk

(a fixture for some time weeks or months)

 

Strategic Spaces - a space that arises from specific community use

Example: A community garden or gathering space

(a something in place for up to a few years)

 

The Built Environment - a space created for long term use

Example: The Chicago Cultural Center Building

(several years or more - long term)

 

Policy & Landmarks - a master plan or destination significant to history

Example:  Reversing the flow of the Chicago River or "The Bean."

(decades or centuries  - really long term)

we operate in between

architecture and people

Whenever a person makes a place their own, through policy or even a type of act or experience, we call it an "urban intervention."  The range and approaches an urban intervention can take are perhaps infinite, and incredibly diverse. They could be fleeting moments, like a street performance or an act in public — or — they could be something as monumental and timeless as reversing the flow of a river, or devoting the entire coast of a city to be a giant park. Regardless of scale, all exist in a specific moment in time, some past, some present, and some extend far into the future.

 

In order to better talk about all of these, it made sense for us to organize them into more specific types - we did this based on time.
