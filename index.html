Cities are more than a collection of buildings.

In order to understand what a city will look like in the future, we need to understand the present. Today is tomorrow's context, and time never stops. The first important piece of information to understand is that cities are more than just the physical parts they are composed of. When we walk out our front doors we are immediately audience to the physical manifestations of human decisions and culture. When we call an Uber, or play Pokemon Go, we are interacting with a network and platform that has spacial elements but exist on a completely different layer than our physical world.

 

Cities are a physical manifestation of a human interaction and culture.

Master plans, infrastructure, parks, bike-lanes, are all examples of planning and design decisions people before us have made to augment how we experience our environment. These are big, expensive, decisions and they’re easy to see once you know how to look for them. But we’d be foolish to think that only people with official titles can make these decisions that impact how we experience the world around us.  Quite often, people without a title or official power, do just that, by taking things into their own hands making their community the way they want it to be.

 

These instances of individual manipulation of their environment are known as 'Urban Interventions'. They are the physical act of developing a community or augmenting space to better suite specific needs.

Urban interventions are a contemporary example of organic place-making.

On the micro scale, they are the bleeding edge of a city’s growth and change

They are the smallest physical

change due to human presence that retains a city’s essence.

The Living City

The point at which a city is alive, adaptive, and responsive to changes in real-time.

By studying the rate that several archetypal urban experiences become technologically integrated in cities, we can begin to form a specific picture of what those cities will look like in the future, and how the inhabitants will collaborate in the process of place-making and developing a community.
