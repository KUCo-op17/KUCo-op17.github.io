// LAWRENCE, KS // YYYY.MM.DD

Other than transportation, consumer access to goods has a huge impact on how our urban fabric would function. I wanted to focus on how these actions could be automated, and how that would function. That is what brought me to A Drone-Slinging UPS Van Delivers the Future. I have always been fascinated by the potential of drone technology, however do its infancy there are more questions than solutions to its problems. One of the first things that come to mind when considering what the possibility of drones is their potential in package delivery. Before reading the article it correctly assumed I imagined amazon parking centers found at a measurable distance from one another where droves would simply dispatch out. But I find the concept of the drone van to be an interesting concept. It would shorten the amount of time for each package to be delivered from the truck saving the driver from having to park, get out, find the package, walk to the door, and drive again; to simply have the driver either stop and allow the drone to do its thing or drive at a slow enough pace for the drone to be able to resettle. However it does not come with the tag line of 30 minutes or less.

Max Cantu-Lima

// Chicago, IL // YYYY.MM.DD

“Small-scale interventions are characterized by their community-focus and realistic goals”

 

This article speaks with Mike Lydon, who co-authored Tactical Urbanism 2: Short-Term Action, Long Term Change  (available on PDF). Its purpose is to help inform people that they have the power to alter their city environment through the same urban interventions our team has been focusing on. Mike says a beautiful line regarding the process of change through these small actions:

 

“The goal is not to simply do a cool project that will get cleaned up by the city or thrown away, but to make something – even something temporary – that will change how a place works and is perceived. And once that change has been made, to figure out how it can be made again or made permanent.”

 

What caught my attention is how these instances exist within time. That no matter how large the scale may be, the permanence is determined by the people, the local government, their relationship, the quality of said project, ect. The most important thing about these initiatives is “Knowing and responding to locals needs” to convey a message to the people and organizations that can come together to make lasting change.

TACTICAL URBANISM (PDF)

Patrick Griffin

// Chicago, IL // 2017.09.01

One of the favored arguments made by those who deny the idea that climate change is happening and that people are the primary cause is that they aren't for or against electric, its just that they're pro-all forms of energy. This argument is dumb for a multitude of reasons, mainly that from a fiscal standpoint, it makes no sense to continue to invest in a form of power that runs off of limited resources when a renewable source is cheaper and the technology requires less maintenance. But, to follow the argument further, people who believe in this line of thought often also argue that if we are going 'green' why isn't China or India doing the same?

 

Firstly, China and India have not been industrialized nearly the same time the United States has been. Secondly, we have put 3x more carbon in the atmosphere than anyone else so we are 3x more responsible and have 3x the duty of everyone else to be an example for what can happen. Thirdly, and this is where the articles come in to play - These two countries are going to be ahead of us in investing in these new technologies and we're going to get left behind. So as an answer to the rhetorical question I posed above, India and China ARE investing in these things, despite the head-start the United States has had for a long time. Moreover, our investment in a technology generally makes it a safer bet for others, and causes its market share to grow rapidly and securely.

China's Fossil Fuel Ban and an Electric Future
